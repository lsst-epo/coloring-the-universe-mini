Mapping the Milky Way

Introduction

We live inside a galaxy called the Milky Way. Our galaxy contains over 400 billion stars, and we now have a good idea of its size and shape as well as our location within it. But how do we know this? The Milky Way is too big for us to send a space probe outside the galaxy to photograph it in its entirety. We need to use different techniques to figure out what it looks like from our place inside the galaxy.

Imagine you live in a house, and you want to know what it looks like, but you can’t go outside. You could build a model of it by observing the inside of your house and noting the layout and size of the rooms. You could then look out the windows at other houses, to see if any of them have a similar appearance to the model of your house.

We can figure out what the Milky Way looks like in a similar way: by mapping the locations of stars inside our galaxy, and by comparing the shape and size of our galaxy to other galaxies we can observe from a distance. Galaxies, like houses, come in different shapes and sizes. The most common galaxy types are spiral, elliptical, and irregular galaxies.

In this investigation you will learn how to use LSST data to determine the type of galaxy we live in. You’ll also learn how to figure out where in the Milky Way we live. We can do both by mapping the locations of stars in the sky.

When there are lots of stars in an image, it becomes impossible to quantify exactly how many stars are in a particular area just by looking at it. Since LSST will precisely map the locations of about 17 billion stars during its ten-year survey, it will definitely be impossible to count them visually. Using the LSST data for our galaxy

One of the six scenarios above accurately models what type of galaxy the Milky Way is, and where we live in it. To determine which one it is, you will compare the simulated concentration maps to the actual star concentration map of the Milky Way Galaxy created from LSST observations. Studying the LSST star concentration map will be more challenging for two reasons: (1) Our galaxy has more complex structure than the simulated galaxies you just studied. (2) LSST can’t observe all of the sky, only what is visible from its location in the Southern hemisphere. The areas of the sky that are not observed by LSST are shown as white.

The Milky Way concentration map is made from the number of stars that appear in y-band filter images. The y-band filter allows LSST to observe infrared light from stars. Since infrared light is not blocked by dust clouds, the y-band filter allows us to see more stars than we would observe by using only visible light wavelengths.
